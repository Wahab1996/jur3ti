Gastric Ulcer

Definition

A gastric ulcer is a type of peptic ulcer that occurs in the gastric mucosa, typically along the lesser curvature between the corpus and antrum. It represents a mucosal defect ≥ 0.5 cm in diameter penetrating through the muscularis mucosae.

Etiology

  • Helicobacter pylori infection (most common cause worldwide)
  • NSAID use (impairs prostaglandin synthesis and mucosal protection)
  • Excess gastric acid secretion (less common than in duodenal ulcers)
  • Stress-related mucosal damage (e.g., severe illness, burns)
  • Smoking, alcohol, and corticosteroid use (contributory factors)

Clinical Features

  • Epigastric pain – classically worsens shortly after meals.
  • Pain described as burning or gnawing; may radiate to the back.
  • Associated symptoms: belching, bloating, nausea, or early satiety.
  • Patients may experience weight loss due to food avoidance.

In contrast, duodenal ulcer pain often improves with food and leads to weight gain.

Complications

  • Bleeding – from erosion into the left gastric artery (hematemesis or melena).
  • Perforation – presenting with sudden severe abdominal pain, rigidity, and pneumoperitoneum.
  • Penetration – ulcer extends into adjacent organs (e.g., pancreas).
  • Gastric outlet obstruction – due to edema or fibrosis from chronic ulcers.
  • Malignant transformation – up to 5–10% risk; biopsy always indicated.

Diagnosis

Initial Evaluation

  • Assess for risk factors: NSAID use, H. pylori, smoking, alcohol.
  • Basic labs: CBC (for anemia), metabolic panel, and fecal occult blood test if bleeding suspected.

Noninvasive Tests

  • H. pylori testing: urea breath test or stool antigen test.

Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD)

  • Gold standard for diagnosis.
  • Allows direct visualization and biopsy to rule out malignancy.
  • Benign ulcers: smooth base, regular margins.
  • Malignant ulcers: irregular, heaped-up margins with nodularity.

Biopsy Indications

  • All gastric ulcers (to exclude carcinoma).
  • Multiple biopsies from ulcer edges and base, and additional samples for H. pylori detection.

Treatment

General Measures

  • Discontinue NSAIDs and avoid alcohol and tobacco.
  • Encourage small, frequent meals and avoid eating before bedtime.

Pharmacologic Therapy

IndicationTreatment
H. pylori positiveEradication therapy: PPI + 2 antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin + clarithromycin) for 14 days.
H. pylori negativePPI monotherapy for 8 weeks.
NSAID-inducedStop NSAID; PPI for 8–12 weeks or use misoprostol if NSAID must be continued.

Supportive Medications

  • PPIs: Omeprazole 20–40 mg daily for ≥8 weeks.
  • H2 blockers: Famotidine 20 mg twice daily (alternative if PPI not tolerated).
  • Sucralfate: 1 g four times daily – forms a protective mucosal barrier.

Follow-Up

  • Repeat EGD with biopsy to confirm healing and rule out malignancy.
  • Ensure eradication of H. pylori if previously positive.
  • Monitor for recurrence if symptoms persist or risk factors remain.

Prevention

  • Avoid NSAIDs and smoking.
  • Use PPIs or misoprostol for patients requiring chronic NSAID therapy.
  • Limit alcohol and caffeine intake.
