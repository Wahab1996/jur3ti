Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Definition

Chronic kidney disease is defined as an abnormality of kidney structure or function that persists for more than 3 months and affects overall health.

Common Causes

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Hypertension
  • Chronic glomerulonephritis
  • Polycystic kidney disease
  • Prolonged obstruction or recurrent infections

Pathophysiology

Progressive nephron loss leads to compensatory hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons, causing further damage, glomerulosclerosis, and tubular atrophy. The decline in GFR results in fluid, electrolyte, and metabolic disturbances.

Clinical Presentation

  • Asymptomatic in early stages
  • Fatigue, pruritus, anorexia in advanced stages
  • Signs of fluid overload: peripheral edema, dyspnea
  • Uremic symptoms: confusion, nausea, metallic taste

Diagnostic Evaluation

Screening and Diagnosis

  • Screen high-risk patients (e.g., diabetes, hypertension) annually.
  • Diagnosis is based on reduced eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m² or markers of kidney damage for >3 months.

Imaging

  • Renal ultrasound: small echogenic kidneys suggest chronic disease.
  • CT or MRI if structural abnormality suspected.

Complications

  • Anemia of CKD
  • Electrolyte imbalance (hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis)
  • CKD–Mineral and Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD)
  • Volume overload and hypertension
  • Uremic pericarditis, encephalopathy
  • Increased risk of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease

Management

1. General Principles

  • Treat the underlying cause (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, GN).
  • Avoid nephrotoxic agents (NSAIDs, contrast media, aminoglycosides).
  • Maintain euvolemia and balanced nutrition.

2. Pharmacologic Therapy

  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs: slow progression by reducing intraglomerular pressure.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: recommended in diabetics with CKD.
  • Statins: reduce cardiovascular risk.
  • Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs): treat anemia when Hb < 10 g/dL.
  • Phosphate binders, vitamin D analogues: manage CKD-MBD.

3. Lifestyle and Nutrition

  • Low-sodium, low-protein diet (0.6–0.8 g/kg/day if not on dialysis).
  • Manage blood pressure to <130/80 mmHg.
  • Encourage smoking cessation and regular exercise.

4. Renal Replacement Therapy

Indicated for end-stage renal disease (eGFR < 15 or refractory complications). Options include:

  • Hemodialysis
  • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Renal transplantation

Monitoring and Follow-up

  • Monitor BP, renal function, and electrolytes regularly.
  • Adjust drug doses according to eGFR.
  • Screen for cardiovascular risk factors.
