Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

Definition

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder characterized by recurrent abdominal pain associated with alterations in bowel habits (stool frequency, form, or appearance) without structural or biochemical abnormalities.

Epidemiology

  • Very common in North America and Europe.
  • More prevalent in women than men.
  • Onset typically in young adulthood.

Pathophysiology

  • Altered gastrointestinal motility and visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Disturbances in the gut–brain axis.
  • Changes in gastrointestinal permeability and microbiota.
  • Possible triggers: infection, stress, diet, psychological factors.

Clinical Presentation

  • Recurrent abdominal pain at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with two or more of the following:
    • Related to defecation.
    • Change in stool frequency.
    • Change in stool form (appearance).
  • May be associated with bloating, mucus in stool, or sensation of incomplete evacuation.
  • Symptoms often worsen with stress or certain foods.

Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis requires all of the following:

  • Recurrent abdominal pain, on average, ≥1 day per week in the last 3 months.
  • Associated with ≥2 of:
    • Pain related to defecation.
    • Change in stool frequency.
    • Change in stool form.
  • Symptom onset ≥6 months before diagnosis.

Subtypes (Based on Predominant Stool Pattern)

SubtypeStool Pattern
IBS-CConstipation-predominant
IBS-DDiarrhea-predominant
IBS-MMixed type (both constipation and diarrhea)
IBS-UUnclassified

Diagnostic Workup

  • Clinical diagnosis after excluding organic causes.
  • Basic evaluation:
    • Complete blood count (CBC).
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) or fecal calprotectin to exclude IBD.
    • TSH if hypothyroidism suspected.
    • Celiac serology (anti-TTG IgA) if diarrhea-predominant.
  • Alarm features that warrant further investigation:
    • Unintentional weight loss.
    • Rectal bleeding or anemia.
    • Family history of colon cancer, IBD, or celiac disease.
    • Onset after age 50.

Treatment

1. Nonpharmacologic Therapy

  • Reassurance and patient education.
  • Identify and avoid dietary triggers (e.g., caffeine, fatty foods, lactose, FODMAPs).
  • High-fiber diet (for IBS-C) or low-FODMAP diet (for global IBS symptoms).
  • Psychological interventions: cognitive-behavioral therapy, stress reduction, relaxation training.

2. Pharmacologic Therapy

Symptom TargetMedication Options
Constipation (IBS-C)Laxatives (polyethylene glycol), lubiprostone, linaclotide.
Diarrhea (IBS-D)Loperamide, bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine), rifaximin, eluxadoline.
Abdominal pain / global symptomsAntispasmodics (hyoscine, dicyclomine), low-dose tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs (for pain modulation).

Note: Pharmacologic choices should be individualized based on predominant symptoms and patient tolerance.

Prognosis

  • Chronic, fluctuating course.
  • No increased risk of colorectal cancer or mortality.
  • Quality of life may be significantly impacted; multidisciplinary care is often beneficial.
