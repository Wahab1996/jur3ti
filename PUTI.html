Pediatric Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)

Definition

A **urinary tract infection (UTI)** is an infection involving the bladder, urethra, ureters, and/or kidneys. It is one of the most common bacterial infections in infants and children.

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

  • More common in females (shorter urethra).
  • In uncircumcised males under 1 year, risk is increased.
  • Other risk factors:
    • Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)
    • Constipation or dysfunctional voiding
    • Urinary tract obstruction or congenital anomalies (CAKUT)
    • Catheter use or neurogenic bladder

Etiology

Most common organism: Escherichia coli (70–90%)

  • Others: Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterococcus, Pseudomonas
  • Viruses (e.g., adenovirus) → hemorrhagic cystitis (rare)

Clinical Presentation

Age GroupCommon Findings
Infants (<2 years) Fever without focus, irritability, poor feeding, vomiting, failure to thrive, jaundice (in neonates)
Older children Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain (cystitis), flank pain (pyelonephritis), new-onset incontinence
Adolescents Typical UTI symptoms ± systemic illness (fever, chills, flank tenderness)

Types of Pediatric UTI

TypeMain SiteKey Features
Cystitis (Lower UTI)BladderDysuria, frequency, suprapubic pain, no fever
Pyelonephritis (Upper UTI)KidneyFever, flank pain, vomiting, systemic illness
Asymptomatic bacteriuriaBladder (colonization)No symptoms, positive culture

Diagnosis

1. Urine Collection

  • Infants: catheterized specimen or suprapubic aspiration
  • Toilet-trained: clean-catch midstream urine
  • Urine bag samples – not reliable for culture (used for screening only)

2. Urinalysis (UA)

  • Leukocyte esterase and nitrite → suggest UTI
  • Microscopy: pyuria ≥5 WBC/hpf, bacteriuria

3. Urine Culture

  • Gold standard for diagnosis.
  • Positive if ≥50,000 CFU/mL of a single organism (catheter/suprapubic specimen).
  • Use to guide antibiotic therapy.

4. Imaging (when indicated)

  • Renal and bladder ultrasound (RBUS): after first febrile UTI or if atypical course.
  • Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG): if ultrasound abnormal or recurrent febrile UTI (to assess for VUR).
  • DMSA scan: optional for assessing renal scarring in recurrent pyelonephritis.

Treatment

Empiric Antibiotic Therapy (based on clinical presentation)

TypePreferred RouteEmpiric ChoicesDuration
Cystitis (lower UTI) Oral Amoxicillin-clavulanate, 2nd/3rd gen cephalosporin (cefixime, cefpodoxime), TMP-SMX (if local resistance <20%) 3–5 days (older child)
Pyelonephritis (febrile UTI) IV (initially), then oral step-down Ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, or gentamicin → switch to oral when afebrile 7–14 days
Neonates IV Ampicillin + gentamicin or cefotaxime 10–14 days

Supportive Care

  • Hydration, fever control (acetaminophen/ibuprofen)
  • Address constipation and bladder dysfunction

Prevention & Follow-Up

  • Encourage adequate hydration and frequent voiding.
  • Treat constipation and avoid holding urine.
  • Teach proper perineal hygiene (front-to-back wiping).
  • Consider antibiotic prophylaxis for:
    • Recurrent febrile UTI
    • Vesicoureteral reflux (grades III–V)
  • Reevaluate after treatment with repeat urine test only if symptoms persist or recurrence suspected.

Complications

  • Renal scarring → hypertension, chronic kidney disease
  • Sepsis (especially in infants)
  • Recurrent UTI or vesicoureteral reflux
