Primary Dysmenorrhea

Definition

Primary dysmenorrhea refers to recurrent lower abdominal and pelvic pain that occurs shortly before or during menstruation, in the absence of pelvic pathology. It is the most common cause of menstrual pain in adolescents and young women.

Pathophysiology

The condition is caused by increased production of **endometrial prostaglandins (especially PGF₂α)** during menstruation, leading to:

  • Hyperactive uterine contractions
  • Uterine vasoconstriction → ischemia
  • Stimulation of pain receptors in the uterus

Clinical Features

  • Cyclic, crampy lower abdominal and/or pelvic pain beginning 6–12 months after menarche
  • Occurs just before or with onset of menstruation, lasting 8–72 hours
  • Pain may radiate to the back or thighs
  • Associated symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, headache, fatigue
  • Severity remains stable from cycle to cycle

Unilateral or progressively worsening pain should raise suspicion for secondary dysmenorrhea (e.g., endometriosis, adenomyosis).

Diagnosis

  • Clinical diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Typical features: cyclic pain, onset soon after menarche, no abnormal pelvic findings.
  • Pregnancy test: always indicated in reproductive-age patients.
  • Pelvic exam or imaging is not required in adolescents with typical symptoms.

If symptoms are refractory to treatment or atypical, evaluate for secondary causes with ultrasound or gynecologic referral.

Treatment

1. First-line Therapy

  • NSAIDs (first choice): start 1–2 days before expected menses and continue 2–3 days into menstruation.
    • Ibuprofen 400 mg every 6 hours as needed
    • Naproxen 250–500 mg every 12 hours as needed
  • Mechanism: inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis.

2. Hormonal Therapy

  • Combined hormonal contraceptives (COCs, patch, or ring)
  • Progestin-only contraception (DMPA, implant, IUD)
  • Continuous use of COCs can provide faster relief than cyclic use.

3. Non-Pharmacologic Measures

  • Heat therapy (e.g., heating pad on lower abdomen)
  • Exercise and physical activity
  • Sleep hygiene and stress reduction

4. Refractory Cases

  • Check adherence to NSAIDs and hormonal therapy.
  • Evaluate for secondary dysmenorrhea (e.g., endometriosis, PID, fibroids).
  • Referral to gynecology for further assessment (e.g., laparoscopy if needed).

Note: Opioids are not recommended for management of dysmenorrhea.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Endometriosis: chronic pelvic pain, dyspareunia, infertility
  • Adenomyosis: enlarged tender uterus, menorrhagia
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): fever, vaginal discharge, cervical motion tenderness
  • Ovarian cysts or torsion: unilateral pain, acute onset

Patient Education

  • Reassure that this is a common and benign condition.
  • Encourage early and consistent NSAID use each cycle.
  • Discuss contraceptive benefits of hormonal therapy if used.
  • Advise follow-up if pain worsens or new symptoms appear.
