Anemia and Its Common Types

Definition & Overview

Anemia is defined as a decrease in hemoglobin or hematocrit below the normal range for age and sex, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.

Classification is based on mean corpuscular volume (MCV):

  • Microcytic: MCV < 80 fL (Iron deficiency, Thalassemia, Chronic disease)
  • Normocytic: MCV 80–100 fL (Acute blood loss, Hemolysis, Chronic disease, CKD)
  • Macrocytic: MCV > 100 fL (Vitamin B12 or Folate deficiency)

General Diagnostic Approach

  • Step 1: CBC → Confirm anemia, note MCV.
  • Step 2: Reticulocyte count → Low = underproduction; High = loss/hemolysis.
  • Step 3: Peripheral blood smear (PBS).
  • Step 4: Targeted tests: iron studies, vitamin B12, folate, kidney/liver/thyroid function.

Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

Etiology

  • Chronic blood loss (GI bleeding, menorrhagia)
  • Increased demand (pregnancy, growth)
  • Reduced intake or absorption (celiac disease, gastrectomy)

Lab Findings

ParameterIDA
Serum Iron↓
Ferritin↓
TIBC↑
Transferrin saturation↓
RDW↑

Treatment

  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., GI bleed, menstrual loss).
  • Oral iron: Ferrous sulfate 325 mg PO daily–TID (provide 65 mg elemental iron per tablet).
  • Take with vitamin C; avoid with tea, calcium, PPI.
  • Continue 3–6 months after Hb normalization.

Anemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)

Pathophysiology

Inflammatory cytokines increase hepcidin → ↓ iron absorption and sequestration in macrophages → impaired erythropoiesis.

Lab Findings

ParameterACD
Serum Iron↓
Ferritin↑
TIBC↓
Transferrin saturation↓ or normal

Treatment

  • Treat underlying chronic disease (e.g., infection, autoimmune, malignancy).
  • Consider erythropoietin-stimulating agents in CKD.
  • Iron therapy only if proven concomitant IDA.

Megaloblastic Anemia (Vitamin B12 / Folate Deficiency)

Etiology

  • B12 deficiency: Pernicious anemia, gastrectomy, ileal resection, vegan diet.
  • Folate deficiency: Poor diet, alcoholism, pregnancy, methotrexate.

Clinical Features

  • Glossitis, fatigue, pallor.
  • B12 deficiency → neurological signs (paresthesia, ataxia).

  • B12: IM 1000 µg daily for 1 week, then weekly × 1 month, then monthly.

Thalassemia

Overview

Genetic defect in globin chain synthesis leading to microcytic, hypochromic anemia. Common in Mediterranean, Asian, and Middle Eastern populations.

Diagnostic Clues

  • Microcytosis disproportionate to degree of anemia.
  • Normal/high ferritin and iron.
  • Normal RDW (vs. IDA where RDW is ↑).
  • Family history or ethnic predisposition.

Confirmatory Test

Hemoglobin electrophoresis:

  • ↑ HbA₂ in β-thalassemia trait
  • ↓/normal HbA₂ in α-thalassemia

Treatment

  • Trait/minor: No specific therapy.
  • Major/intermedia: Regular transfusions + iron chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine).
  • Consider folate supplementation and genetic counseling.
