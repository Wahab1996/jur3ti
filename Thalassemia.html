Approach to Thalassemia in Primary Health Care (PHC)

1. Initial Assessment

  • Obtain detailed family and ethnic history (Mediterranean, Asian, or African ancestry increases risk).
  • Perform complete blood count (CBC) and peripheral smear.
  • Identify microcytic anemia with normal or elevated ferritin levels (helps differentiate from iron deficiency).
  • If thalassemia suspected, refer for hemoglobin electrophoresis or genetic testing.

2. Counseling and Screening

  • Provide genetic counseling to patients and families.
  • Screen family members and reproductive partners for thalassemia carrier status.
  • Preconception counseling for at-risk couples to prevent major thalassemia births.

3. Management in PHC

Thalassemia Minor (Trait)

  • Usually no treatment required.
  • Educate patients to avoid unnecessary iron supplementation unless iron deficiency confirmed.
  • Prescribe folic acid supplementation during pregnancy or intercurrent illness.

Thalassemia Intermedia and Major

  • Transfusion therapy is the mainstay for moderate to severe cases.
  • Indications:
    • Hemoglobin (Hb) < 7 g/dL or clinical symptoms (fatigue, poor growth).
    • Target Hb: maintain 9–10 g/dL.
  • Iron overload management: chelation therapy with agents such as deferasirox when ferritin ≥ 1000 µg/dL.
  • Monitor for complications: gallstones, splenomegaly, bone deformities.
  • Refer to tertiary care for:
    • Persistent anemia or iron overload despite treatment.
    • Splenectomy consideration for hypersplenism or massive splenomegaly.
    • Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation or gene therapy evaluation (curative potential).

Supportive Care

  • Provide routine immunizations (especially post-splenectomy).
  • Encourage balanced nutrition and folate-rich diet.
  • Monitor for endocrinopathies, diabetes, and cardiac complications in long-term follow-up.

4. Special Situations

Pregnancy

  • High-risk pregnancy—refer to specialist care early.
  • Preconception genetic counseling is essential.
  • Maintain Hb > 10 g/dL to prevent intrauterine growth restriction.
  • Use deferoxamine (chelation) only in 2nd and 3rd trimesters.

Post-Splenectomy Care

  • Vaccinate against pneumococcus, meningococcus, and Haemophilus influenzae type B.
  • Educate regarding risk of overwhelming post-splenectomy sepsis (OPSI).
  • Consider lifelong antibiotic prophylaxis as per protocol.

5. Referral Criteria from PHC

  • Severe anemia unresponsive to standard management.
  • Progressive splenomegaly.
  • Iron overload complications (liver, cardiac involvement).
  • Pregnancy in thalassemia major or intermedia.

6. Patient Education and Follow-Up

  • Emphasize the genetic nature of the disease.
  • Encourage regular follow-up every 3–6 months.
  • Advise avoiding unnecessary iron therapy unless iron deficiency is proven.
