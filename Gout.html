Acute Gout Attack: Management and Follow-up (Family Medicine)

Initial Evaluation

  • Rule out septic arthritis and other acute monoarthritis causes.
  • Assess the need for joint aspiration or imaging if the diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Provide adequate pain control and rest; apply ice locally.

Acute Management

  • Nonpharmacologic: Rest the affected joint; apply ice packs.
  • Pharmacologic therapy (start within 24 hours):
    • First-line options: NSAIDs, colchicine, or glucocorticoids.
    • Combination therapy: Consider if ≥4 joints or severe pain.
    • Continue existing urate-lowering therapy if the patient is already on it.

Drug Details

  • NSAIDs: Naproxen, indomethacin, or ibuprofen for 3–5 days (shortest effective duration). Avoid in renal/hepatic impairment or peptic ulcer disease.
  • Colchicine: Best if started within 12 hours of onset. Reduce dose in CKD or hepatic impairment. Common side effects: diarrhea, nausea.
  • Glucocorticoids: Prednisone or prednisolone for 5 days or tapered regimen; intra-articular injections if 1–2 accessible joints.

Follow-up and Long-Term Management

  • Reassess within 1–2 weeks to confirm resolution of symptoms.
  • Consider starting urate-lowering therapy (ULT) if:
    • ≥2 flares per year
    • Tophi present
    • CKD stage ≥2
    • Uric acid nephrolithiasis
  • Lifestyle and comorbidity management:
    • Limit alcohol, red meat, shellfish, and high-fructose corn syrup.
    • Encourage weight loss if overweight.
    • Screen and manage sleep apnea, hypertension, and diabetes.
    • Use losartan preferentially; avoid hydrochlorothiazide.

Family Medicine Follow-up Plan

  • Review serum uric acid levels 2–4 weeks after initiating or adjusting ULT.
  • Monitor renal and hepatic function if on long-term therapy.
  • Provide education on early recognition and self-management of flares.
  • Coordinate care with rheumatology for recurrent or refractory cases.
