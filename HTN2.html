Hypertension (HTN)

Definition

  • In adults: Persistent systolic blood pressure (SBP) ≥ 130 mm Hg and/or diastolic blood pressure (DBP) ≥ 80 mm Hg (ACC/AHA 2017), or SBP ≥ 140 mm Hg and/or DBP ≥ 90 mm Hg (ISH, JNC 8).
  • In children: BP ≥ 95th percentile or ≥ 130/80 mm Hg, whichever is lower.

Classification

  • Normal: SBP < 120 mm Hg AND DBP < 80 mm Hg
  • Elevated: SBP 120-129 mm Hg AND DBP < 80 mm Hg
  • Stage 1 HTN: SBP 130-139 mm Hg OR DBP 80-89 mm Hg
  • Stage 2 HTN: SBP ≥ 140 mm Hg OR DBP ≥ 90 mm Hg

Epidemiology

  • Affects ⅓ to ½ of adults in the US.
  • Primary HTN: 90% of adult cases, increasing in children/adolescents.
  • Prevalence rises with age and is higher in African Americans.

Etiology

  • Primary (essential) hypertension: Multifactorial (genetic, epigenetic, environmental).
  • Secondary hypertension: Has an identifiable cause (e.g., kidney disease, endocrine disorders).

Risk Factors

  • Positive family history, race, age (non-modifiable)
  • Obesity, diabetes, high salt/low potassium diet, smoking, inactivity, alcohol, stress (modifiable)

Clinical Features

  • Usually asymptomatic.
  • If symptoms: headaches (esp. in morning), dizziness, tinnitus, blurred vision, flushed face, palpitations, nervousness, fatigue.

Subtypes

  • White coat HTN: Elevated in clinic only.
  • Masked HTN: Normal in clinic, elevated outside.
  • Isolated systolic HTN: SBP ≥ 140 mm Hg, DBP normal.

Physical Examination

  • Multiple BP readings (different arms, different times).
  • BMI, waist circumference, heart, and neurological exam.

Diagnosis

  • Confirm with ambulatory/home BP measurement.
  • Assess for end-organ damage and secondary causes.
  • Initial workup: glucose, electrolytes, kidney function, lipids, urinalysis, ECG.

Investigations

  • Routine labs: Fasting glucose, kidney function (serum creatinine/eGFR), electrolytes, lipids, urinalysis.
  • Additional: Echocardiography, fundoscopy, sleep studies (if suspicious for OSA), renal artery imaging (if secondary HTN suspected).

Management

Lifestyle Modifications (all patients)

  • Weight loss (most effective)
  • DASH diet (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low saturated fat)
  • Reduce sodium (≤ 1,500 mg/day)
  • Increase potassium
  • Limit alcohol
  • Regular aerobic and resistance exercise
  • Smoking cessation

Pharmacologic Therapy

  • Initial treatment based on stage, comorbidities, patient’s race.
  • First-line: Thiazide diuretics, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers.
  • Black patients: Thiazide or CCB preferred.
  • Add-on/second-line: Beta blockers, loop diuretics, aldosterone antagonists, direct vasodilators, others as needed.
  • Special notes: Avoid ACEI + ARB combination. Adjust therapy based on comorbidities (CKD, diabetic nephropathy, heart failure, stroke).

Monitoring & Follow-Up

  • Assess medication adherence, side effects, BP control, labs regularly.
  • Titrate therapy as needed.

Complications

  • Cardiovascular: LV hypertrophy, MI, heart failure, stroke, aortic aneurysm/dissection, PVD, AFib
  • Renal: CKD, proteinuria, nephrosclerosis
  • Ophthalmologic: Hypertensive retinopathy (Keith-Wagener-Barker scale)

Special Populations

  • Children: Diagnosis based on percentiles; primary more common in US youth.
  • Elderly: Isolated systolic HTN more common due to arterial stiffness.
  • Pregnant women: Refer to dedicated guidelines.

Prevention

  • Maintain healthy weight, diet, exercise regimen, avoid excess salt/alcohol/smoking.
