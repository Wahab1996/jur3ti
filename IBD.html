Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) comprises two chronic, relapsing inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal tract: Crohn disease (CD) and Ulcerative colitis (UC). Both share overlapping features but differ in their distribution, depth of inflammation, and extraintestinal manifestations.

Crohn Disease (CD)

Overview

Chronic, relapsing inflammatory disorder that can affect any part of the GI tract — from the mouth to the anus — most commonly the terminal ileum and colon.

Etiology & Pathophysiology

  • Immune dysregulation with inappropriate activation against gut microbiota.
  • Genetic predisposition (e.g., NOD2 mutation).
  • Environmental triggers: smoking, NSAIDs, infections.

Clinical Features

  • Chronic diarrhea (often non-bloody).
  • Right lower quadrant abdominal pain.
  • Weight loss, fatigue, fever.
  • Perianal disease: fistulas, abscesses, fissures.

Extraintestinal Manifestations

  • Joints: arthritis, spondylitis.
  • Skin: erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum.
  • Eyes: uveitis, episcleritis.
  • Hepatobiliary: cholelithiasis, fatty liver.
  • Renal: nephrolithiasis (calcium oxalate).

Diagnosis

  • Colonoscopy: skip lesions, cobblestone mucosa, deep linear ulcers.
  • Histology: transmural inflammation, granulomas (noncaseating).
  • Imaging: MRE/CTE – bowel wall thickening, fistulas, creeping fat.
  • Serology: ↑ ASCA, pANCA negative.
  • Labs: ↑ CRP, anemia, low B12, iron deficiency.

Complications

  • Fistulas (enteroenteric, enterocutaneous).
  • Abscess formation.
  • Strictures → bowel obstruction.
  • Malabsorption and vitamin deficiencies.
  • Increased risk of colorectal cancer.

Treatment

  • Induction of remission: corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone, budesonide).
  • Maintenance therapy: immunomodulators (azathioprine, methotrexate), biologics (anti-TNF agents).
  • Antibiotics: for perianal disease or abscesses (ciprofloxacin + metronidazole).
  • Surgery: for strictures, obstruction, or refractory disease (not curative).

Ulcerative Colitis (UC)

Overview

Chronic inflammatory disease limited to the colon and rectum, characterized by continuous mucosal inflammation starting in the rectum and extending proximally.

Etiology & Pathophysiology

  • Autoimmune-mediated mucosal inflammation limited to the large intestine.
  • Triggered by gut flora imbalance, infections, and immune dysregulation.
  • Smoking is protective (unlike Crohn disease).

Clinical Features

  • Bloody diarrhea with mucus.
  • Fecal urgency and tenesmus.
  • Lower abdominal pain relieved by defecation.
  • Systemic symptoms: fatigue, low-grade fever.

Extraintestinal Manifestations

  • Joints: arthritis, sacroiliitis, ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Skin: erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum.
  • Eyes: uveitis, iritis, episcleritis.
  • Hepatobiliary: primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC).

Diagnosis

  • Colonoscopy: continuous lesions, friable mucosa, loss of vascular pattern, ulcerations.
  • Histology: mucosal and submucosal inflammation, crypt abscesses.
  • Serology: ↑ pANCA, ASCA negative.
  • Labs: ↑ ESR/CRP, anemia, hypoalbuminemia.

Complications

  • Toxic megacolon.
  • Massive bleeding.
  • Perforation.
  • Colorectal carcinoma (risk increases after 8–10 years).

Treatment

  • Mild–moderate: 5-ASA agents (mesalamine oral or rectal).
  • Moderate–severe: corticosteroids for induction, then immunomodulators (azathioprine, 6-MP) or biologics.
  • Refractory/complicated: colectomy is curative.
