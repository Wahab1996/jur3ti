Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI)

Definition

Upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) involve the structures **at or above the vocal cords** — including the nasal cavity, sinuses, pharynx, and larynx. Common examples include:

  • Common cold (viral rhinitis)
  • Acute rhinosinusitis
  • Pharyngitis
  • Laryngitis
  • Influenza
  • Croup (in children)
  • COVID-19 (viral URTI with systemic potential)

Etiology

  • Viral (most common): Rhinovirus, coronavirus, adenovirus, parainfluenza, RSV, influenza.
  • Bacterial (rare): Group A Streptococcus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae (often secondary infection).

Epidemiology

  • Occurs year-round but peaks in fall and winter.
  • More frequent in children (average 6–8 episodes/year).
  • Spread by respiratory droplets and contact with contaminated surfaces.

Clinical Features

  • Nasal congestion and rhinorrhea.
  • Sore throat or mild pharyngitis.
  • Cough (often due to postnasal drip).
  • Low-grade fever, malaise, myalgia (more common in influenza).
  • Hoarseness or laryngitis in some cases.
  • Symptoms typically resolve within 7–14 days.

Diagnosis

  • Usually clinical based on symptoms and examination.
  • Investigations are generally unnecessary unless complications or differential diagnoses suspected:
    • Rapid antigen test / throat culture: if streptococcal pharyngitis suspected.
    • Influenza or COVID-19 testing: in appropriate season or epidemiologic context.
    • Sinus imaging: if symptoms >10 days, severe facial pain, or high fever (suspect bacterial sinusitis).

Management

General Principles

  • URTIs are typically viral and self-limited.
  • Antibiotics are not indicated unless bacterial infection is proven or highly suspected.
  • Supportive treatment is the cornerstone of management.

Symptomatic Treatment

  • Hydration and rest.
  • Saline nasal irrigation or steam inhalation for congestion.
  • Analgesics/antipyretics: paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • Decongestants (pseudoephedrine, oxymetazoline ≤5 days).
  • Antitussives if cough is disturbing (dextromethorphan, honey in children >1 year).
  • Lozenges or warm saline gargles for sore throat.

When to Consider Antibiotics

  • Confirmed or strongly suspected bacterial pharyngitis (positive rapid test or Centor ≥3).
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis (symptoms >10 days, double worsening, high fever).
  • Otitis media in children with severe symptoms or under 2 years.

Patient Education and Prevention

  • Hand hygiene and avoiding close contact with sick individuals.
  • Avoid unnecessary antibiotic use.
  • Vaccination:
    • Annual influenza vaccine.
    • COVID-19 vaccination per local guidelines.
  • Encourage smoking cessation (reduces URTI and secondary infections).

Complications (usually rare)

  • Secondary bacterial infection (sinusitis, otitis media, pneumonia).
  • Exacerbation of asthma or COPD.
  • Postinfectious cough or reactive airway disease.
