Common Headache Types and Migraine

Overview

Headache is one of the most frequent complaints in primary care. Most cases represent primary headaches—tension-type, migraine, or cluster—while secondary headaches stem from underlying causes (e.g., infection, vascular, or structural pathology).

Acute Management

  • Tension-type: NSAIDs or acetaminophen. Chronic cases may need amitriptyline prophylaxis.
  • Migraine: NSAIDs for mild attacks; triptans for moderate–severe episodes.
  • Cluster: 100% oxygen + subcutaneous or intranasal triptan (e.g., sumatriptan).

Migraine: Clinical Overview

Migraine is a chronic neurovascular disorder characterized by recurrent attacks of unilateral, pulsating headache often accompanied by nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia. Some patients experience preceding aura symptoms (visual, sensory, or speech disturbances).

Diagnostic Criteria

  • ≥ 5 attacks fulfilling all of the following:
    • Duration 4–72 hours (untreated or unsuccessfully treated).
    • ≥ 2 of:
      • Unilateral location
      • Pulsating quality
      • Moderate or severe pain intensity
      • Aggravation by routine physical activity
    • During headache: nausea/vomiting or photophobia + phonophobia.

Common Migraine Subtypes

  • With aura: focal neurological symptoms precede headache (visual scintillations, sensory changes, dysphasia).
  • Without aura: most common type; typical headache without preceding neurological signs.
  • Chronic migraine: headache ≥15 days/month for >3 months, with ≥8 days showing migraine features.
  • Menstrual migraine: occurs around menstruation due to estrogen drop; may require triptan prophylaxis (frovatriptan, naratriptan).
  • Vestibular migraine: episodic vertigo associated with migraine history.
  • Hemiplegic migraine: transient unilateral motor weakness + typical aura (familial or sporadic).
  • Retinal migraine: transient monocular visual loss or scintillations.

Migraine Management

Acute Treatment

  • Reduce stimuli (quiet, dark environment).
  • NSAIDs, acetaminophen, or aspirin ± caffeine for mild cases.
  • Triptans (e.g., sumatriptan, rizatriptan) for moderate–severe attacks.
  • Antiemetics: metoclopramide or prochlorperazine if nausea present.
  • IV fluids if dehydrated or vomiting.

Non-Pharmacologic Measures

  • Maintain regular sleep, hydration, and meals.
  • Avoid triggers (hormonal fluctuations, certain foods, alcohol).
  • Behavioral therapy: biofeedback, relaxation techniques.
  • Keep a headache diary for pattern recognition.

Red Flags Suggesting Secondary Headache (“SNOOP”)

  • S: Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)
  • N: Neurological deficits
  • O: Onset sudden (“thunderclap”)
  • O: Older age at onset (>50 years)
  • P: Progression/change in pattern

If any present → perform urgent neuroimaging (CT/MRI) ± lumbar puncture as indicated.
