Diabetic Foot Examination

1. Introduction

The diabetic foot examination helps detect neuropathy, ischemia, and infection early to prevent ulceration and amputation.

Tip: Always explain the procedure and ensure both feet are exposed and clean.

2. Inspection

  • Inspect both feet, including the soles and between toes.
  • Look for:
    • Skin changes: dryness, cracking, calluses.
    • Ulcers, infection, or deformities.
    • Footwear wear pattern and fit.

3. Palpation

  • Check skin temperature (compare both feet).
  • Assess for tenderness or swelling.
  • Palpate pedal pulses:
    • Dorsalis pedis artery.
    • Posterior tibial artery.

4. Sensory Examination (Neuropathy Assessment)

  • Monofilament test: Apply a 10 g monofilament at 10 sites per foot.
  • Vibration sense: Use a 128 Hz tuning fork on the great toe.
  • Light touch and proprioception if indicated.

5. Reflexes

  • Test Achilles (ankle) reflexes bilaterally.
  • Loss indicates possible peripheral neuropathy.

6. Vascular Assessment

  • Check pedal pulses and compare both feet.
  • Look for signs of ischemia: pallor, rubor, hair loss.
  • Measure ankle-brachial index (ABI) if needed.

7. Functional and Footwear Assessment

  • Ask about walking, balance, or aids used.
  • Inspect shoes for:
    • Abnormal wear.
    • Foreign objects or rough seams inside.

8. Documentation and Education

  • Record findings: neuropathy, ischemia, deformity, ulcers.
  • Classify risk level (low, moderate, high).
  • Educate patient on:
    • Daily foot inspection.
    • Proper footwear use.
    • Reporting any new lesions.

9. Summary

A complete diabetic foot exam should be performed at least once a year for all diabetic patients to identify early neuropathy or vascular compromise.
