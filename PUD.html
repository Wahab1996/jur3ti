Gastric and Duodenal Ulcers

Both gastric and duodenal ulcers are part of peptic ulcer disease (PUD) — mucosal defects that penetrate through the muscularis mucosa due to the imbalance between gastric acid and mucosal defense.

Gastric Ulcer

Definition

A peptic ulcer located in the gastric mucosa, typically along the lesser curvature between the corpus and antrum.

Etiology

  • H. pylori infection
  • Chronic NSAID use
  • Smoking and alcohol use
  • Stress-related mucosal injury

Clinical Features

  • Epigastric pain worsens after eating.
  • Pain described as burning or gnawing.
  • Often associated with early satiety and nausea.
  • Patients may lose weight due to food avoidance.

Diagnosis

  • EGD: visualizes ulcer and allows biopsy to exclude malignancy.
  • Benign: smooth, regular margins.
  • Malignant: irregular, nodular edges — always biopsy.

Treatment

  • H. pylori positive: Triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin).
  • H. pylori negative: PPI for 8 weeks.
  • NSAID-related: Discontinue NSAID; use PPI or misoprostol.

Complications

  • Bleeding (left gastric artery)
  • Perforation → peritonitis
  • Gastric outlet obstruction
  • Malignant transformation (5–10%)

Follow-Up

  • Repeat endoscopy to confirm healing and rule out cancer.

Duodenal Ulcer

Definition

A peptic ulcer of the duodenal mucosa, usually located in the duodenal bulb (first portion of the duodenum).

Etiology

  • H. pylori infection (most common cause).
  • Excess gastric acid secretion (Zollinger-Ellison syndrome rare).
  • NSAID use.
  • Smoking, stress, and chronic disease.

Clinical Features

  • Epigastric pain improves with eating and recurs 2–5 hours later.
  • Pain may awaken patient at night (nocturnal pain).
  • Weight gain possible due to relief with food.

Diagnosis

  • EGD: confirms ulcer and rules out complications.
  • Biopsy only if lesion appears atypical or suspicious for malignancy (rare).
  • Noninvasive H. pylori testing recommended in all cases.

Treatment

  • H. pylori positive: Eradication therapy (PPI + 2 antibiotics).
  • Acid suppression: PPI for ≥4 weeks.
  • Stop NSAIDs and smoking.

Complications

  • Bleeding (posterior wall → gastroduodenal artery)
  • Perforation (anterior wall → peritonitis)
  • Penetration into pancreas or biliary tract
  • Gastric outlet obstruction (chronic scarring)

Follow-Up

  • Repeat EGD only if symptoms persist or complications suspected.
  • No routine biopsy required for typical duodenal ulcers.
