Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) – Management Approach

1. Initial Steps

  • Perform ABCDE assessment.
  • Obtain urgent diagnostics:
    • Point-of-care (POC) glucose
    • Basic metabolic panel (BMP)
    • Blood gas analysis
  • Assess volume status.

2. Principal Interventions

  • Fluid resuscitation: Start with 0.9% NaCl or lactated Ringer’s solution.
  • Potassium repletion: Begin if baseline K⁺ ≤ 5.0 mEq/L. Maintain K⁺ between 4–5 mEq/L.
  • Insulin therapy: Initiate short-acting IV insulin when K⁺ > 3.5 mEq/L.
  • Sodium bicarbonate: Consider only if severe refractory metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.0).
  • Identify and treat precipitating causes (e.g., sepsis, myocardial infarction).
  • Avoid rapid correction of serum sodium and osmolality to prevent cerebral edema.
  • Consult endocrinology and consider ICU admission.

3. Noninsulin Diabetes Medications

  • Avoid noninsulin agents in patients with type 1 diabetes mellitus.
  • Stop SGLT2 inhibitors.
  • For type 2 diabetes, other agents may be considered with endocrinology input.

4. Monitoring

  • Check vital signs, mental status, and hydration hourly.
  • POC glucose every 1–2 hours until resolution of hyperglycemia.
  • Blood gas and BMP (including electrolytes) every 2–4 hours.
  • For DKA: measure serum β-hydroxybutyrate every 4 hours.
  • For HHS: monitor serum osmolality every 2–4 hours.
  • Monitor for signs of cerebral edema (headache, mental status changes, seizures).

5. Disposition

  • Admission: Indicated for all patients with HHS and most with DKA.
  • ICU admission: If severe DKA/HHS, hemodynamic instability, or altered mental status.
  • Discharge criteria (mild DKA):
    • Acidosis resolved
    • No serious precipitating cause
    • Tolerates oral intake
    • Stable on basal-bolus insulin regimen
    • Reliable follow-up possible

Key Notes

Maintain serum potassium between 4–5 mEq/L.

Avoid rapid correction of hyponatremia to prevent osmotic demyelination syndrome.

Rapid fall in serum osmolality can lead to cerebral edema.

Special Consideration

Pregnant patients with DKA require joint management by an endocrinologist and obstetrician due to high maternal-fetal risk.
