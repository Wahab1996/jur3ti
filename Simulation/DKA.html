Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) Management

Adult DKA Management (≥18 years)

Step 1: Initial Assessment

  1. Perform ABCDE assessment.
  2. Obtain urgent diagnostics:
    • POC glucose
    • Basic metabolic panel (BMP)
    • Blood gas analysis
  3. Assess hemodynamic and volume status.

Step 2: Fluid Resuscitation

  1. Start with 0.9% NaCl or lactated Ringer’s solution.
  2. Typical initial rate: 500–1000 mL/hour for the first 1–2 hours.
  3. Adjust based on clinical response, hemodynamics, and corrected sodium.

Step 3: Potassium Management

  1. If K⁺ ≤ 5.0 mEq/L → begin potassium replacement.
  2. Maintain serum K⁺ between 4–5 mEq/L.
  3. Do not start insulin until K⁺ > 3.5 mEq/L.

Step 4: Insulin Therapy

  1. Begin IV short-acting insulin infusion once K⁺ > 3.5 mEq/L.
  2. Target glucose fall: 50–75 mg/dL per hour.
  3. Add 5% dextrose to IV fluids when glucose < 250 mg/dL.

Step 5: Bicarbonate Therapy (If Indicated)

  1. Consider only if pH < 7.0 and refractory to fluids/insulin.
  2. Use cautiously due to risk of worsening hypokalemia.

Step 6: Monitoring

  • Vital signs, mental status, and hydration: hourly.
  • POC glucose: every 1–2 hours.
  • BMP, electrolytes, and blood gas: every 2–4 hours.
  • Watch for signs of cerebral edema: headache, confusion, seizures.

Step 7: Disposition

  • Most patients require hospital admission; consider ICU for severe cases or hemodynamic instability.
  • Discharge possible if:
    • Acidosis resolved
    • Stable on oral intake and basal-bolus insulin regimen
    • Reliable follow-up available

Avoid rapid correction of serum sodium or osmolality — risk of cerebral edema or osmotic demyelination.

Pediatric DKA Management (<18 years)

Step 1: Initial Assessment

  1. Assess ABCs and obtain baseline labs:
    • POC glucose
    • Venous blood gas and electrolytes
    • BUN/creatinine
  2. Assess hydration status and mental state (risk of cerebral edema).

Step 2: Fluid Therapy

  1. Initial bolus: 10–20 mL/kg of 0.9% NaCl over 1 hour (max 1 L).
  2. Reassess perfusion; additional boluses may be needed if still in shock.
  3. Maintenance + deficit replacement: Use weight-based rates (1.5–2× maintenance over 48 hours).
  4. Avoid overly rapid rehydration to prevent cerebral edema.

Step 3: Potassium Replacement

  1. Start K⁺ replacement once urine output is adequate.
  2. Target: Maintain serum potassium 4–5 mEq/L.

Step 4: Insulin Therapy

  1. Do not give insulin bolus.
  2. Begin IV insulin infusion at 0.05–0.1 units/kg/hour after first hour of fluid therapy.
  3. Add dextrose (5–10%) when glucose < 250 mg/dL, continue insulin until ketosis and acidosis resolve.

Step 5: Monitoring

  • Neurological checks hourly (look for signs of cerebral edema).
  • POC glucose hourly until stable, then every 2 hours.
  • Electrolytes and venous blood gas every 2–4 hours.

Step 6: Management of Cerebral Edema (Emergency)

  • Reduce fluid rate by one-third.
  • Administer hypertonic saline (3% NaCl, 5–10 mL/kg over 30 min) or mannitol (0.5–1 g/kg IV over 20 min).
  • Urgent transfer to PICU.

Step 7: Transition and Disposition

  • Switch to subcutaneous insulin once:
    • DKA resolved (pH > 7.3, HCO₃⁻ > 15 mEq/L)
    • Patient can tolerate oral intake
  • All pediatric DKA cases should be managed in a hospital, preferably in the PICU.

Rapid rehydration or insulin bolus increases cerebral edema risk — proceed gradually.
