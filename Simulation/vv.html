🩸 Anemia Simulation Case (Family Medicine)

Case Title

Evaluation and Management of Anemia in Primary Care

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize the clinical features of anemia.
  • Classify anemia by MCV and identify possible causes.
  • Formulate an appropriate diagnostic and management plan.
  • Identify red flags requiring referral or hospital admission.

Case Presentation

Patient: 45-year-old female

Chief Complaint: Fatigue and shortness of breath on exertion for two months.

History

  • No history of acute bleeding.
  • Heavy menstrual periods (7–8 days).
  • No weight loss or fever.
  • Diet unchanged.

Physical Examination

  • Conjunctival pallor
  • Heart rate: 98 bpm
  • BP and temperature: Normal
  • No jaundice, hepatomegaly, or splenomegaly

Initial Laboratory Results

Simulation Timeline

  1. Phase 1 – Presentation: Patient presents with fatigue and pallor. Learners take focused history and perform examination.
  2. Phase 2 – Workup: Learners order CBC, MCV, reticulocyte count, ferritin, and consider differential diagnoses.
  3. Phase 3 – Diagnosis: Identify iron deficiency anemia (microcytic, hypochromic).
  4. Phase 4 – Management: Discuss treatment plan (oral iron, dietary advice, treat menorrhagia).
  5. Phase 5 – Reflection: Debrief on diagnostic reasoning, patient communication, and prevention strategies.

Learner Roles

  • Resident/Doctor: Lead consultation, interpret labs, formulate plan.
  • Nurse: Measure vitals, support patient education.
  • Patient (Standardized): Responds with fatigue, dizziness, heavy menses history.
  • Observer/Facilitator: Guides discussion, ensures learning objectives are met.

Discussion & Assessment Questions

  1. What type of anemia is most likely in this case?
  2. What is the most common underlying cause?
  3. What other tests are indicated (e.g., stool for occult blood, TSH, B12)?
  4. When is transfusion indicated?
  5. What are the key elements of patient education?

Management Summary

  • Treat the underlying cause (e.g., iron deficiency from menorrhagia).
  • Oral iron supplementation (ferrous sulfate 325 mg TID).
  • Transfusion if Hb ≤ 7 g/dL (or ≤ 8 g/dL in cardiac disease).
  • Address heavy menstrual bleeding and nutrition.

  • What key findings helped you classify the anemia?
  • How did you decide which investigations were necessary?
  • What communication strategies did you use to discuss iron therapy and adherence?
  • How can similar cases be managed efficiently in a primary care setting?
