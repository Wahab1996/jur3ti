Hemolytic, Aplastic, and Sickle Cell Anemias

Hemolytic Anemia

Hemolytic anemia refers to premature destruction of red blood cells (RBCs), resulting in shortened RBC lifespan and increased reticulocyte production. Causes are classified as intrinsic (inherited) or extrinsic (acquired).

Classification

CategoryExamplesMechanism
Intrinsic (RBC defect)G6PD deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis, sickle cell disease, thalassemiaMembrane, enzyme, or hemoglobin defect
Extrinsic (external factor)Autoimmune hemolytic anemia, microangiopathic (TTP/HUS/DIC), infections (malaria), mechanical (valves)Immune or mechanical destruction

Key Laboratory Findings

  • ↑ Reticulocyte count
  • ↑ Indirect bilirubin
  • ↑ LDH
  • ↓ Haptoglobin
  • Peripheral smear: spherocytes, schistocytes, bite cells, or sickled cells (depending on cause)

G6PD Deficiency

  • X-linked enzyme defect → RBCs susceptible to oxidative stress.
  • Triggered by infection, fava beans, or drugs (sulfa, dapsone, primaquine).
  • Smear: "bite cells" and "Heinz bodies".
  • Diagnosis: low G6PD enzyme activity (after acute episode resolves).
  • Treatment: avoid triggers, supportive care during crisis.

Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia (MAHA)

RBC fragmentation from endothelial injury and microthrombi (e.g., TTP, HUS, DIC). Schistocytes on smear are characteristic.

  • TTP: ADAMTS13 deficiency → treat with plasma exchange.
  • HUS: Shiga toxin (E. coli O157:H7) → supportive care.
  • DIC: Widespread coagulation activation → treat underlying cause.

Management Summary

  • Treat the underlying cause.
  • Transfusion as needed (careful in AIHA).
  • Folic acid supplementation.
  • Glucocorticoids for immune-mediated types.
  • Avoid oxidative drugs in G6PD deficiency.

Aplastic Anemia

Aplastic anemia is bone marrow failure characterized by pancytopenia (↓ RBCs, WBCs, platelets) and hypocellular marrow without infiltration or fibrosis.

Etiology

  • Idiopathic/autoimmune (most common)
  • Drugs: chloramphenicol, chemotherapy, NSAIDs, carbamazepine
  • Radiation or toxins (benzene)
  • Viral infections (EBV, parvovirus B19, hepatitis, HIV)
  • Inherited syndromes (Fanconi anemia)

Clinical Features

  • Fatigue, pallor (anemia)
  • Infections (neutropenia)
  • Easy bruising, bleeding (thrombocytopenia)
  • No splenomegaly (helps differentiate from other causes)

Diagnosis

  • CBC: Pancytopenia with normal MCV.
  • Reticulocyte count: ↓.
  • Bone marrow biopsy: Hypocellular marrow replaced by fat.
  • Exclude other causes (vitamin deficiency, leukemia, myelodysplastic syndrome).

Treatment

  • Remove offending agents or treat infection.
  • Supportive: transfusions, antibiotics for neutropenia.
  • Immunosuppressive therapy: antithymocyte globulin (ATG) + cyclosporine.
  • Allogeneic stem cell transplantation (preferred in young patients with severe disease).

Sickle Cell Disease (SCD)

A hereditary hemoglobinopathy caused by a point mutation in the β-globin gene, leading to production of HbS. Under deoxygenated conditions, HbS polymerizes → RBC deformation (“sickling”) → vaso-occlusion and hemolysis.

Genotypes

  • Sickle cell anemia (HbSS): homozygous; most severe form.
  • Sickle cell trait (HbAS): heterozygous; usually asymptomatic, normal life expectancy.
  • Compound heterozygotes: HbSC, HbSβ-thalassemia (variable severity).

Clinical Features

  • Chronic hemolytic anemia (fatigue, pallor, jaundice)
  • Vaso-occlusive pain crises (bone pain, chest pain, abdomen)
  • Dactylitis (hand-foot swelling in infants)
  • Splenic sequestration, autosplenectomy (functional asplenia by age 6)
  • Infections by encapsulated organisms (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae)
  • Acute chest syndrome: new pulmonary infiltrate + pain + hypoxia
  • Stroke (especially in children), leg ulcers, priapism

Diagnosis

  • Peripheral smear: sickled cells, target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies.
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis: confirms presence of HbS.
  • Newborn screening: universal in many countries.

Management

  • Hydroxyurea: increases HbF → reduces crises and need for transfusion.
  • Folic acid supplementation.
  • Vaccination: pneumococcal, meningococcal, H. influenzae type B, influenza.
  • Prophylactic penicillin from 2 months to 5 years of age.
  • Manage pain crises: IV fluids, oxygen, NSAIDs/opioids as appropriate.
  • Blood transfusion for severe anemia or acute chest syndrome.
  • Exchange transfusion for stroke or severe vaso-occlusive complications.
  • Curative: bone marrow transplantation (selected severe cases).

Complications

  • Stroke
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Leg ulcers
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Avascular necrosis (femoral head)
  • Retinopathy
