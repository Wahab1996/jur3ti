Shingles (Herpes Zoster)

Shingles (herpes zoster) is a painful, blistering rash occurring along a dermatome due to reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). After a person has chickenpox, VZV remains dormant in the dorsal root ganglia and can reactivate later in life, especially in immunocompromised or older individuals.

Key Points

  • Cause: Reactivation of latent VZV (same virus that causes chickenpox).
  • Symptoms:
    • Dermatomal, unilateral rash of grouped vesicles on an erythematous base
    • Severe burning or stabbing pain (neuropathic)
    • Possible fever or malaise before rash onset
  • Complications:
    • Postherpetic neuralgia (persistent nerve pain)
    • Encephalitis
    • Ocular involvement (herpes zoster ophthalmicus) – may lead to blindness
    • Facial paralysis and hearing loss (herpes zoster oticus)
  • Diagnosis: Usually clinical; PCR testing of vesicle fluid if unclear.
  • Treatment:
    • Antivirals: acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir (start within 72 hours)
    • Analgesics: for pain relief
  • Prevention:
    • Recombinant zoster vaccine (RZV) recommended for:
      • All individuals ≥ 50 years old
      • Immunocompromised adults ≥ 19 years old
